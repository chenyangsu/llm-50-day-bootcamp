50-Day LLM Bootcamp

From backpropagation to a model you built, fine-tuned, and evaluated honestly. 24 July – 11 September 2026.

What this is

Fifty consecutive days, each sized for three to four focused hours. It follows one rule the whole way through: you do not get to skip backpropagation, tokenization, or attention. Those three are where people who “know LLMs” turn out not to, and they are cheap to learn properly now and expensive to fake later.

The first four weeks you build everything yourself — an autograd engine, a bigram model, a byte-pair encoder, a decoder-only transformer. Only after that do you touch Hugging Face, because the library is much easier to reason about once you already know what it is hiding. Weeks five through seven cover the pretrained ecosystem, scaling laws, post-training, retrieval, and evaluation, carried by one applied project on PubMed abstracts that starts on day 33 and is still running on day 50.

How a day works

Every day page has the same seven parts, in this order:

Part What it’s for
Why today One sentence on what this day unlocks. Read it before you start.
What you should be able to do tonight Two or three checkable claims. Not “understand attention”.
Tasks Three to five items with minute estimates. Tick them off; the page remembers.
Resources Every link the day needs. Nothing you have to go hunting for.
Deliverable The artifact you should have at the end — a file, a notebook, a plot, a paragraph.
Checkpoint One question. If you can’t answer it without notes, you are not done.
Prepare for tomorrow Ten minutes tonight. This is what the evening email sends you.

The seven phases

Days 1–7 #### Foundations Linear algebra, calculus, autograd. Karpathy’s micrograd, built twice — the second time from an empty file. Softmax regression as multinomial logistic regression. A two-layer MLP with a backward pass you wrote yourself.

Days 8–14 #### Language models N-grams, perplexity and smoothing before any neural machinery. Then makemore parts 1–3: a bigram count table and a one-layer network computing the same thing, then an MLP, then how to debug a network that trains badly.

Days 15–21 #### Tokens and attention Manual backprop through a whole network. BPE implemented from scratch and trained on your own PubMed corpus. Then the transformer on paper — the textbook chapter and the original paper — with no transformer code yet.

Days 22–28 #### Build a GPT Follow Karpathy through a decoder-only transformer, then rebuild it with the video closed. Sweep the hyperparameters and report a table. Read nanoGPT and diff it against yours. Milestone 1.

Days 29–35 #### Pretrained models The Hugging Face course, chapters 1–3. Encoder versus decoder, and when an encoder is still right. The applied project starts: PubMed abstracts, a prompting baseline first, then a fine-tune. Milestone 2.

Days 36–42 #### Scaling and post-training GPT-3, scaling laws, Chinchilla — read critically, and redo the compute arithmetic yourself. InstructGPT for why a base model isn’t an assistant. LoRA, with your hands on it.

Days 43–50 #### Pipeline, RAG, evaluation DPO. nanochat end to end, which is the only place you see all the stages at once. Retrieval over your abstracts. Then evaluation, which is the part that matters most for clinical work. Capstone on day 50.

Five rules that make this work

  1. Reproduce, don’t watch. A Karpathy lecture you watched is worth roughly nothing. Type it out, run it, break it. If a day’s tasks say “rebuild from an empty file”, that instruction is the day.
  2. Change one thing. After you reproduce something, change the model width, the context length, the learning rate, or the sampling temperature, and predict what happens before you run it. Being wrong is the useful part.
  3. Produce the deliverable. Every day names an artifact. If you finish a day with nothing on disk, the day did not happen.
  4. Answer the checkpoint out loud. It takes ninety seconds and it is the only honest signal you have about whether you learned the thing or watched someone else know it.
  5. Catch-up days are load-bearing. Days 7, 14, 21, 28, 35, 42 and 49 are deliberately not full. They exist because you will fall behind somewhere, and a plan with no slack is a plan you abandon in week three. If you are on track, take the day off or go deeper. Do not pull work forward into them by default.

The evening email

Every evening at about 8:10 pm you get one email: what tomorrow is, and the two-to-four things worth doing tonight so tomorrow starts clean — download the PDF, clone the repo, start the dataset downloading, confirm the GPU is awake. Nothing in that email takes more than ten minutes. It is the same content as the Prepare for tomorrow block at the bottom of each day page.

Start here

Tonight: set up your environment — Python, PyTorch, a working GPU or Colab runtime, and two accounts. About an hour, and it is the difference between day 1 being a study day and day 1 being an afternoon of dependency errors.

Then: Day 1, or the full 50 days.
